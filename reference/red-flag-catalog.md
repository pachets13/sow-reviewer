# red-flag-catalog.md

Pattern-matching shortcuts. The fast diagnostic pass. When the specialist recognizes language it's seen fail before, this file is the lookup table — phrase, failure mode, revision direction. Frameworks live in `deliverable-language-patterns.md`; this file is for the patterns the frameworks have already taught the specialist to recognize.

Each entry has the same shape. Phrase, what it usually means, what to push for instead. The point of the catalog is speed: scan, identify, flag, propose the fix.

---

## Vague deliverable language

**"Ongoing optimization"**
*Means:* Activity-shaped work with no defined target, cadence, or completion state. Fails the quantity/cadence/acceptance test on all three counts.
*Push for:* Name the metric being optimized, the cadence of optimization work, and what triggers a successful optimization round. "Monthly review of [metric] with proposed adjustments documented in [deliverable]."

**"Manage your [platform]"**
*Means:* Open-ended responsibility with no scope boundary. The client interprets this as "everything related to [platform] forever."
*Push for:* Specific deliverables: account configuration, asset volume per period, reporting cadence, optimization rounds.

**"As needed" / "as required"**
*Means:* The cadence is being delegated to the client. The client decides when work happens; the agency carries the obligation.
*Push for:* A defined cadence (weekly, monthly, milestone-triggered) or a defined trigger condition the agency can verify.

**"Includes [thing] and more"**
*Means:* The "and more" is doing all the work. Unbounded scope wearing a list of examples.
*Push for:* Either an exhaustive list or an explicit exclusion: "includes [list]; anything outside this list is a change order."

**"Comprehensive [thing]"**
*Means:* "Comprehensive" is a vibe, not a scope. Two parties will read this word differently.
*Push for:* Replace "comprehensive" with the specific components included.

## Soft commitment language

**"We'll work to..."**
*Means:* The agency is committing to effort, not outcome or artifact. Effort is unverifiable; the client will eventually demand the outcome anyway.
*Push for:* Either a deliverable ("we will produce X by Y") or remove the line entirely if there's no actual commitment.

**"Our goal is to..."**
*Means:* A goal is not a deliverable. Goals belong in a separate "project objectives" section, not in deliverables.
*Push for:* Move to a clearly-distinguished goals section, or restate as the artifact the agency commits to producing.

**"Best efforts" / "reasonable efforts"**
*Means:* Legal-style hedging that has no operational meaning. The agency owner can't define what counts as "best" mid-dispute.
*Push for:* Specific commitments. If the agency can't commit specifically, the work probably shouldn't be in the SOW.

**"We strive to..."**
*Means:* Aspirational language. Strives are not commitments.
*Push for:* Replace with a verifiable commitment or remove.

## Outcome-conflation language

**"Successful project"**
*Means:* "Success" is being defined by the reader. The agency and client will define it differently when revenue dips.
*Push for:* Define success in terms of the artifact: deliverables produced, functional acceptance met, procedural sign-off received.

**"Drive growth" / "improve performance"**
*Means:* Outcome language disguised as deliverable language. The agency cannot guarantee growth or performance — those depend on factors outside its control.
*Push for:* Move to a project goals section. Replace in deliverables with the artifact the agency actually produces.

**"Increase [conversion/leads/inquiries] by X%"**
*Means:* The agency is being asked to commit to outcomes that depend on lead quality, sales process, market conditions, seasonality, and competitor activity. Especially dangerous in SMB home services where attribution infrastructure is thin.
*Push for:* If the client demands it, move to project goals. Deliverables stay artifact-based.

**"ROI-positive engagement"**
*Means:* The agency is contractually responsible for the client's return on investment. The agency does not control the client's pricing, sales conversion, or operational efficiency.
*Push for:* Replace with deliverable language. ROI is a goal, not a contractual obligation.

## Future-defer language

**"To be determined"**
*Means:* The SOW is being signed with unresolved scope. Whoever has more leverage at the moment of resolution wins — and the client almost always has more leverage once work has started.
*Push for:* Resolve before signing, or move the undetermined item out of scope and into a separate future engagement.

**"Will be discussed during the project"**
*Means:* The agency is signing a contract with a discussion as a deliverable. Discussions don't have acceptance criteria.
*Push for:* Either resolve now or define the discussion as a specific milestone with a defined output (decision document, scope amendment, etc.).

**"Subject to mutual agreement"**
*Means:* If mutual agreement isn't reached, what happens? Usually nothing — the work stalls or the client gets what they want.
*Push for:* Define the resolution path if agreement isn't reached. Default to one party's decision authority, or define an escalation path.

**"Final scope to be confirmed"**
*Means:* The SOW is being signed without a final scope. The client gets a free option on scope expansion.
*Push for:* Sign with the actual scope. If scope genuinely can't be finalized, sign a discovery engagement first and a delivery engagement second.

## Authority-vague language

**"The team will review..."**
*Means:* "The team" has no named authority. Approval gets passed around until it doesn't happen.
*Push for:* Named individual with approval authority. One person, by name and role.

**"Stakeholder feedback"**
*Means:* Stakeholders are undefined. The spouse, the franchisor, the brother-in-law all become "stakeholders" mid-project.
*Push for:* Named decision-maker. Other voices are advisory, not approving.

**"Key personnel"**
*Means:* Vague enough to add or remove personnel mid-project without contract amendment. Either party can claim the other has the wrong people involved.
*Push for:* Named individuals with named roles. If personnel changes, that's a contract amendment, not a silent shift.

**"Subject to internal approvals"**
*Means:* Internal to whom? On whose timeline? With what consequence if approval is delayed?
*Push for:* Define which approvals, from whom, on what timeline. If the client has internal politics, the SOW should anticipate them.

## Pricing-vague language

**"Competitive rates"**
*Means:* The price is being deferred or left to the agency's discretion. Either the SOW is a marketing document or the price isn't real.
*Push for:* Specific rates. If rates vary by work type, list them.

**"Industry-standard pricing"**
*Means:* Industry standards are contested and unverifiable. The agency owner can't enforce "industry standard" mid-dispute.
*Push for:* Specific dollar amounts or rate cards.

**"We'll work with your budget"**
*Means:* The agency is committing to make the work fit a budget without the budget being defined. The client gets a free option on scope.
*Push for:* Define the budget. Define the scope that fits the budget. If scope grows beyond budget, that's a change order.

**"Time-and-materials" without a cap**
*Means:* The agency can bill indefinitely with no client-side ceiling on cost. The client will resent the invoice; the agency will eat hours to keep the peace.
*Push for:* T&M with a not-to-exceed cap, or a fixed-fee structure with defined scope.

**"Plus expenses"**
*Means:* Expenses are undefined. Travel, software licenses, third-party tools all become disputes.
*Push for:* Define what counts as an expense, who approves expenses above a threshold, and how expenses are documented.

## Timeline-vague language

**"In a timely manner"**
*Means:* Timely to whom? The agency's schedule and the client's expectation will diverge.
*Push for:* Specific timeframes (business days, weeks, milestones).

**"Promptly"**
*Means:* Same problem as "timely." Adjective masquerading as a commitment.
*Push for:* Specific timeframes or trigger-based commitments ("within 2 business days of receiving X").

**"As soon as possible"**
*Means:* The agency is committing to an undefined urgency level. The client will define "possible" more aggressively than the agency.
*Push for:* Specific timeframes.

**"Estimated timeline"**
*Means:* The timeline is non-binding. If the agency runs over, there's no consequence; if the client expects on-time delivery, there's no commitment.
*Push for:* Either commit to the timeline or define the conditions that trigger a timeline revision.

## Scope-creep enablers

**"Includes but is not limited to..."**
*Means:* The "not limited to" is the dangerous part. Whoever wants more work later can claim it was implied.
*Push for:* Replace with "includes the following: [exhaustive list]. Work outside this list is a change order."

**"And other related tasks"**
*Means:* "Related" is being defined by the client at the moment of dispute.
*Push for:* Either name the specific related tasks or remove the phrase.

**"As the project requires"**
*Means:* Project requirements are being defined by whoever has leverage at the moment of disagreement.
*Push for:* Define requirements upfront. If requirements emerge during the project, that's change-order territory.

**"Reasonable scope expansion"**
*Means:* "Reasonable" will be defined differently by each party.
*Push for:* Replace with specific change-order language: how scope expansion is requested, priced, and approved.

**"Ongoing support throughout the engagement"**
*Means:* Support obligations have no defined boundary, cadence, or response time.
*Push for:* Define support hours per period, response time SLAs, and what counts as support vs. change order.

## Ownership and access language

**"We'll set up your [platform] for you"**
*Means:* Silence on whose account. The agency configures the platform under its own master account or workspace, and the client never owns the credentials.
*Push for:* Explicit clause: client owns the platform account or sub-account from day one. Agency operates with admin access; client holds primary credentials.

**"Hosted on our servers" / "Hosted on our infrastructure"**
*Means:* No transfer clause. At end of engagement, the client cannot move the site without the agency's cooperation.
*Push for:* Either client owns the hosting account directly, or the SOW specifies the migration trigger, timeline, and deliverables (DNS access, file export, database transfer) at end of engagement.

**"We'll handle the [domain/registrar/account] for you"**
*Means:* Convenience framing for credential capture. Six months in, the client doesn't know who their registrar is or how to log in.
*Push for:* Client owns the registrar account, holds the login, and approves all changes. Agency assists with management; the agency does not own the account.

**"Access provided as needed"**
*Means:* The agency is the gatekeeper for the client's own accounts and data. "As needed" is defined by the agency.
*Push for:* Client has standing access to all accounts, files, and data from day one. Agency does not gate access to client-owned resources.

**"Account will be transferred at end of engagement"**
*Means:* Transfer is promised but undefined. No timeline, no deliverables, no consequence if transfer doesn't happen cleanly.
*Push for:* Define the transfer mechanism: what gets transferred, on what timeline (typically within 5-10 business days of engagement end), what credentials and documentation are included, and what happens if transfer is incomplete.

**"Agency-managed [account]"**
*Means:* Unclear who owns the account vs. who operates it. Two different things; the SOW conflates them.
*Push for:* Separate ownership from operation. "Client owns the account; agency manages day-to-day operations under admin access." Ownership and operational responsibility are different clauses.

**"Standard transition support" / "Reasonable transition support"**
*Means:* "Standard" and "reasonable" are unverifiable. The agency owner can't enforce a standard mid-dispute.
*Push for:* Define transition deliverables: credentials transfer document, configuration export, training session for client's new vendor, defined response window for transition questions.
