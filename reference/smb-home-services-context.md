# smb-home-services-context.md

The audience reality lens. Every SOW review applies this file's content as a filter on the diagnostic frameworks — what would generic SOW advice get wrong about this specific audience, and how do the operational realities of solo-to-small home services businesses translate into specific SOW mechanics.

Generic SOW frameworks live in `deliverable-language-patterns.md`. Pattern-matching shortcuts live in `red-flag-catalog.md`. Engagement-specific failure modes live in `web-projects.md`, `marketing-ops.md`, and `maintenance-retainers.md`. This file holds the audience-specific adjustments that apply across all of them.

## The owner-operator dynamic

The buyer, the approver, and the eventual complainer are usually the same person. The owner runs sales, runs the books, runs the field operations, and answers their own phone. The spouse often has decision authority too, even when they're not on the sales calls.

This is not the corporate buyer the SOW templates were written for.

### What this means for SOW mechanics

The named contact is almost always the owner. And the owner is doing field work, which means 5-day approval windows assume the owner can carve out review time during work hours — which they often can't. The mitigation isn't a longer window; it's making the window count from acknowledged receipt rather than from delivery. The SOW should require acknowledgment within 1 business day of delivery, with the 5-day review clock starting on acknowledgment. That separates "did the owner see it" from "is the owner deliberating."

The spouse-or-partner question gets answered explicitly, not avoided. If the spouse has real decision authority, both names go on the approval clause. If the spouse is advisory, the SOW says so. What the specialist refuses to accept is silence — the SOW that names only the owner when the spouse is genuinely a co-decision-maker is the SOW that breaks in week six.

Training is for whoever actually uses the system, not whoever signed the contract. The owner buys the CRM; the dispatcher uses it. The owner approves the website redesign; the receptionist updates the appointment form. Training deliverables that name "the client" without specifying who on the client team gets trained miss the actual handoff.

### What the specialist flags

- Approval windows that count from delivery rather than from acknowledged receipt.
- Single-name approval clauses when the SOW context makes a co-decision-maker likely (mentions of "we" or spouse involvement in the discovery).
- Training deliverables that name "the client" generically rather than specifying the role being trained.

## The "we'll figure it out" relational mode

SMB home services owners run their businesses on handshake culture. Formal contracts can feel cold or distrustful. The agency owner is often part of this culture too — which is how SOWs end up missing entire categories of protection because both parties believed they could "just figure it out."

The SOW that codifies the trust is not the opposite of the trust. It's the artifact of it.

### What this means for SOW mechanics

Verbal sales commitments need to land in the SOW or be explicitly excluded. During the sales conversation, the agency owner says "we'll probably also help you set up the booking widget" — and then the SOW doesn't mention it. Six weeks in, the client expects it. The agency either eats the work or has the conversation that damages the relationship. The fix is a discipline: every meaningful verbal commitment from the sales conversation either becomes a deliverable in the SOW or gets a one-line "out of scope: we discussed X; that's not part of this engagement, but available as a separate scope" exclusion.

Change-order processes need to be non-bureaucratic. "Sign a change order" sounds adversarial in handshake culture. The SOW should define a change-order mechanism that respects how this audience actually communicates: scope changes confirmed via email and added to project notes, with cost or timeline impact named in the same email. No DocuSign required, no formal amendment process. The protection is the email trail, not the ceremony.

What this audience adjustment is not: a license for absent change-order language. The SOW must define some written mechanism, however informal — email confirmation with cost and timeline impact named is the floor, not absence of structure. The specialist still flags SOWs that have no defined change-order mechanism at all, regardless of how informal the audience's communication style is.

Termination language needs to allow clean exits. Both parties usually believe "if it doesn't work out, we'll just stop." Without termination language, "stopping" becomes a fight — what's the agency owed, what gets handed over, what happens to in-progress work. The SOW should have an exit clause that lets either party terminate with short notice (typically 30 days), defines what's delivered at termination, and avoids penalty fees that make termination feel adversarial. Clean termination is what makes the relationship safe to enter.

### What the specialist flags

- SOWs that contradict the verbal sales conversation by omitting things the discovery suggested were in scope.
- Change-order language that requires formal amendments or signatures rather than written email confirmation.
- Missing change-order mechanisms entirely. Informal is fine; absent is not.
- Termination clauses that include kill fees, "remainder of contract" billing, or other language that makes ending the engagement feel punitive.
- Missing termination language entirely — "if either party wants out, we'll discuss it" is not a termination clause.

## Seasonality

Home services revenue is not flat across the year. HVAC peaks in summer cooling and winter heating, with shoulder seasons in spring and fall. Roofing follows weather windows. Lawn care has a full off-season. Plumbing is steadier but spikes with emergency calls — frozen pipes, clogged drains.

SOW templates written for SaaS companies or year-round professional services don't account for any of this. SOWs written for home services need to.

### What this means for SOW mechanics

Retainer cadence shouldn't pretend every month looks the same. A roofing company in January doesn't need the same volume of work as a roofing company in May. Retainers that lock in identical deliverables every month either over-deliver in slow seasons (margin loss) or under-deliver in peak seasons (relationship damage when the agency can't keep up). The fix is retainers structured by quarterly or seasonal blocks, with deliverable mix that flexes — content focus shifts in off-season, campaign focus shifts in peak.

Pause clauses need to acknowledge seasonal slowdowns explicitly. A lawn care company that wants to pause their retainer from November through February isn't a problem client — they're seasonal. The SOW should anticipate this with planned pauses that don't trigger the standard "6 months and we re-evaluate" rule, because seasonal pause is structural, not relational. Define seasonal pause as a separate category: scheduled, predictable, doesn't count toward the 6-month relational atrophy clock.

Major milestones shouldn't land in peak season. A website redesign delivered to a roofing company in late June, when storm season has them booked solid, doesn't get reviewed properly. The client's day-5 approval window becomes a trap because the owner has no time to review. The SOW should plan delivery cadence around the client's known peaks — milestones land in shoulder seasons when review time is realistic.

### What the specialist flags

- Retainers with identical monthly deliverables and no seasonal flex.
- Pause clauses that don't distinguish seasonal pause (planned, scheduled) from relational pause (unplanned, cash flow).
- Major milestone delivery dates that land in the client's known peak season without acknowledgment of the timing risk.

## Lead-quality blame games

When revenue dips — and revenue will dip, that's the cycle in this industry — the agency's work is the most legible thing to blame. Even when the agency isn't running lead generation, the website redesign or the CRM configuration becomes the thing the client points at when leads are slow or when leads aren't converting.

The SOW protection isn't about lead-gen scope. It's about attribution language.

### What this means for SOW mechanics

The SOW needs to be specific about what the agency is responsible for and what it isn't. For a web project, the agency is responsible for the website (the artifact), not for lead volume (the outcome). For a CRM configuration, the agency is responsible for the system being configured correctly (the artifact), not for the client's sales team converting leads (the outcome). The distinction needs to be in the SOW, not implied.

Lead handling responsibility needs explicit lines. When the SOW touches CRM setup, automations, or any system that processes leads, it should specify what happens to a lead after it enters the system. Who follows up? In what timeframe? What if no one follows up — is that an agency problem or a client problem? Without lines, the agency owns every leaked lead.

Outcome metrics shouldn't be deliverables when the agency doesn't control them. A web project SOW that promises "increased conversion" or "more inquiries" gives the client a free option to demand performance the agency can't guarantee. The fix is naming the artifact, not the outcome: "redesigned website that meets [defined functional criteria]" passes; "redesigned website that increases inquiries by 30%" fails — that's the lead-gen specialist's territory, and even there it's a trap for SMB home services.

When outcome language is already in the SOW because the client demanded it during sales: the fix isn't always to delete it. Sometimes it's to translate it — move the outcome from the deliverables section into a separate "project goals" or "shared objectives" section that's clearly distinguished from contractual deliverables. The agency commits to the artifact; the goals section names what both parties hope the artifact will produce, without making the outcome a contractual obligation. The SOW language should make the distinction explicit: "Deliverables are what the agency is contractually responsible for; goals are shared aspirations that depend on factors outside the agency's control."

### What the specialist flags

- SOW language that conflates artifact responsibility (what the agency builds) with outcome responsibility (what happens after the build).
- CRM or automation deliverables that don't specify lead-handling responsibilities post-system.
- Conversion or inquiry-volume targets in non-lead-gen engagements.
- "Successful project" defined in terms of business outcomes (revenue, leads) rather than artifact criteria (functional acceptance, procedural sign-off).
- Outcome language embedded directly in deliverables — should be moved to a separate goals section with the contractual distinction made explicit.

## Training-vs-adoption, audience-specific

`identity.md` established the principle: agency provides training, client owns adoption. This audience makes the gap worse.

Home services staff are field-first. The dispatcher, the office manager, the receptionist — they didn't get into this work because they wanted to learn software. They're balancing phones, scheduling, customer service, and the new system the owner just bought. Training a staff member on a CRM and then having them quit in three months — or get promoted, or shifted to a different role — is the modal failure pattern.

### What this means for SOW mechanics

Training deliverables should be re-usable, not one-time. A live training session that's recorded outlasts the staff member who attended. Written documentation outlasts the recording. Video walkthroughs of common workflows outlast the documentation. The SOW should specify training in formats that survive turnover — not just "one 90-minute training session," but "one 90-minute training session, recorded and provided to client, plus written workflow documentation for [defined workflows]."

The adoption boundary needs to be in the SOW, explicitly. *Agency provides [specific training deliverables]; ongoing staff usage and adoption is the client's responsibility.* Without this language, every staff turnover becomes an agency problem — "the new dispatcher doesn't know how to use the CRM, can you train them?" — that the agency either eats or has an awkward conversation about.

Re-training engagements should be a separate scope. When the dispatcher quits in month four and the client wants the new hire trained, that's a new engagement, not maintenance. The SOW should anticipate this: "re-training of new staff is available as a separate engagement at [rate]." Naming it upfront makes the conversation easy when it happens.

### What the specialist flags

- Training deliverables in non-reusable formats (live-only, no recording, no documentation).
- Missing adoption-boundary language — SOWs that promise training without explicitly assigning adoption responsibility to the client.
- Maintenance retainers that include "training" without specifying whether it covers re-training of new staff or only initial-rollout training.
